How Hard Can It Be To Write A Book?

Published: 5th, September 2025

authoring novels planning

I'm writing a novel, a task that's been a dream for a long time. It's been a stop/start affair with poor consistency and zero planning. Writing and, importantly, finishing a book is no mean feat and yet the basic concept is very simple. You don't need to be a professor in languages or a genius in world-building, it comes down to just getting your imagination down in words. The words don't have to be worthy of the great icons of literature, a book can be short and use simple vocabulary, and still be highly engaging. Difficulties can arise since you might put in a lot of work but don't know how to finish it, you may veer off the path and break your story without realising, or in many cases you may have a story in your head but you don't know where to start, or maybe never start due to time constraints or lack of confidence. Stories can live in our minds for a lifetime, but nothing exists on paper unless you commit to it. You must have sat down with friends and family sharing stories or throwing around ideas and someone says something like...

"That would be a great premise for a book! Have you thought about writing that down?"

...and you reply with something like...

"The thought had crossed my mind, but I've never really given it any serious consideration..."

Many of us have stories that are worth writing down, whether real or fictional. To start trying to write a book you have to do just that, start writing. For me it began when I was given a beat-up old laptop with nothing on it apart from a word processor and some free games. I had no real use for this machine and while staring at it one day, I had a sudden urge to write something.

That was 20 years ago... the book started as some rambling nonsense my 18 year old self thought was funny, and stubbornly the manuscript has followed me until this day in an unfinished state being carted around on storage devices, lost multiple times, recovered, written out by hand at one point, and then re-typed back into the word processor.

Currently it sits at 80k+ words, has a logical plot with beginning / middle / end, and has an array of odd characters living in modern day London. I have persisted with trying to finish this book on and off now for a ludicrously long time which has shown me one thing; I really want to be a writer! I want to stop dreaming and give this some serious consideration.

So kindly let an amateur author with a near complete dog end of a book share their experience, and maybe offer some (hopefully) useful advice if you are thinking of writing a book yourself.

How I Got Started

I had no story. I had no characters. I had no plan. I had a blank page and a keyboard.

I've always loved reading; from Goosebumps books as a kid to Terry Pratchett, from Russian masters like Dostoevsky and Bulgakov to Irish classics by Flann O'Brian and Donleavy (Irish-American) and many other weird and interesting authors. I believe reading well improves your chances of writing well.

I read books that satisfy my own imagination, and that in turn has helped inspire me to explore my imagination to see what it can conjure up.

Getting started came down these few points:

  • Reading a lot of books.
  • Having quiet and private space to myself.
  • Not caring too much about what I was writing at the time.
  • Just starting and seeing how it felt.

The fact that 20 years have passed since then may sound like I haven't exactly taken this new found passion on board in any serious way at all, but it has stayed with me, always in the back of my mind. For me, the past few months have a been a breakthrough in getting towards the end, and ironing out the characters and plot.

It doesn't matter how long it takes to write your first book. It will probably take years, but if you want to know if it's something you want to do, you have to get started.

What Kept Me Going

This is an interesting question. If I had such a strong desire to write a book, and had already started one, then why have I stopped so many times, only to half-heartedly keep coming back to then stop again?

I think it's because I have not been trying to impress anyone, I've been doing my own thing in my own time. I wrote when the mood took me, and I never felt an urgency in getting to the end. I think not having any sort of structure or plan facilitated this. If I had the ending in my head, and I knew how each character would develop then I would have a story inside me waiting to burst out. Over the years, I've winged every word and event on the day I sat down in front of my computer.

I'd say the key things that kept me going were:

  • A genuine appreciation of what I had already written.
  • A fun sense of the unknown - what will I write today? I have no idea!
  • A quiet understanding that I wanted to get to the end, regardless of how long it was going to take.

I would say write for yourself. If this is your first book and you're experimenting, don't go thinking this is the start of my new career and I'm going to land a fat publishing contract so this book has to be perfect. Write something that excites you, and that you would want to read.

Mistakes I've Made

I'm not going to roast myself too harshly here. I know my book has issues, but I'm just an amateur having some fun. It goes without saying that writing 19 chapters of text for a story that's been made up on the fly is going to have inconsistencies and errors.

Why am I considering mistakes, when I've stated I am writing this for my own enjoyment, and am not attempting to impress anyone? Surely it's what I wanted to write at the time, and that should be good enough? Yes, there is truth in this, but in the last few months I have decided I want to try and get this thing published. I am looking at "mistakes" from the perspective of the reader who is going to pick holes in the plot and the timeline, and every other aspect of the book for that matter. I'd like it to make logical sense, while keeping as much of my original ramblings as possible.

An honest appraisal of the mistakes I've made would have to include:

  • A complete lack of planning. While this gave me unbounded creative freedom, as the book grew, it turned into a mess of scenes, rather than a coherent storyline building towards a climax.
  • Not knowing the backstory of the characters before starting. This resulted in characters that were not developing in an engaging way.
  • Trying to use flowery language too often. This made sentences too long, producing waffle that stunted the flow of the text.
  • Trying to be funny on virtually every line. The novel is a comic farce of sorts, and this was actually killing the humour rather than adding to it.
  • Writing scenes that take place over weeks or months, but the flow was jumpy and hard to follow.

Going back and seeing these issues has given me more motivation to finish, as I want to produce something that makes sense, at a minimum. Fixing stuff is also helping me drive the story because I can take out bits that don't fit, and add connections to events I created later in the book.

How I'm Fixing My First Novel

I've read the manuscript back and corrected of lot of grammar, tidied up the prose, and cut out parts that didn't stack up. I've rewritten several parts of it based off the mistakes identified in the last section.

The specific methods I'm using to improve the book are:

  • Creating a short survey for each character, detailing their personal bio and role in story.
  • Making a timeline of events.
  • Axing fluff to reduce sentence length so the point hits cleanly.
  • Reducing comic elements in general prose to enhance the humour of the core comic scenes.
  • Planned the ending! I have actually sat down, looked at what I already have, and created an ending. I have a goal, and this is helping to me write more consistently.

Conclusion

To answer the 'How Hard Can It Be To Write A Book?' question, I would say the writing of it is not that difficult. Pure writing with no structure or limits is joyous; I found it therapeutic even to sit and relax and let my thoughts spill on to the page.

Writing a book that has depth of character, a strong plot, and a clear lore is not easy. This is essential what I failed to do on the first run, and now I'm backtracking to glue it all together.

When, not if, I write my next book, I will start with a knowledge of who my characters are, I will start by knowing the ending, and I will design a killer opening chapter to get the reader hooked immediately. Armed with those tools, I will then employ my free-flow style to all the bits in the middle, always having the final destination to guide, and motivate me if I loose focus.

Many thanks for reading.
