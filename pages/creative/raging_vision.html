Raging Vision

Terrified, aching, and moving fast.

Have you ever run so hard you felt yourself start to choke on blood? That iron taste and roaring throat?

Feeling like your body is fuelled on caustic soda. All this pain, just to make it home from the office.

This is the world I live in.

I work seventeen hours a day. I can sleep 3 hours a night if I'm lucky. The additives ensure I can be a productive, functional member of the new society. The drops go straight in the eyes before any food or drink is taken. The body rejects the food over time if not pre-emptively supplemented by the stinging sensation of the drops.

Nearly three decades have passed since the collapse. Our old way of life has been stripped away. Time has taken more innocent lives than I can comprehend.

The Rulers took over the offices, the skyscrapers, the Halls of the Rich and Powerful. Every other building, save these deprived estates in which I live, is rubble.

The air drops on the estate roofs keep us alive. The retinal scanners at the offices keep us on the list of places to drop the packages. No eyes, no drops.

The route from the estate to my nearest office is like a warzone. The partially sighted and blind victims of this new order of autocracy have no choice but to scavenge on what they can take, or face becoming another body in the dust.

Many refused to turn wild, opting for a quick end to what little life they had. Many did not have the courage. Thousands lined the routes from the estates to offices.

It was a gauntlet of survival through the decaying streets of the desperate and starving. The eye drops giving me the energy to run like an animal. I had pure strength in the first few hours. The run took just over an hour in each direction. Needed a solid dose when first waking, and a double hit to make it home.

The work was intricate. Eye strain plagued me and the others. Tiny components, screens, harsh lighting. There were no Rulers, only other workers silently head down. They did not need to oversee us. The threat of cutting off the air drops was enough to make sure we were on time and did our work.

The retinal scanners on every door and workstation flashed orange as we passed. A constant reminder. No retinal scan that day? No food. Simple. Our eyes were our keys to life. No eyes, no life.

I had wanted to save O'Conner. She had been my friend before she turned wild. Now she existed amongst the rubble, feeding on carrion birds, rough vegetation that was hard to digest, and the Unthinkable.

The air drops only provided enough resources for a single person. Retinal scanner protected, one drop per-person. Daily top ups of eye drops. Water. Bread. Milk. Ham. Nothing else. All packaged in small receptacles. O'Conner had lived with me until her eyes failed. We had tried to share but it was not enough for two. I had to decide. Every day now I think of trying to save her. Sometimes I see her staggering towards me on the run. Arms out like the undead.

I've been caught twice. Beaten badly, scratched, and bitten. Managed to twist my ankle and went down in an area with hundreds of them, only just escaped with my life to get in and do most of a shift. There was no air drop that evening.

I wonder how much longer I have. My eyes feel worse every day. When the strain becomes too much, I won't be able to work anymore, and I'll be forced to join O'Conner when the others who live in my estate evict me. The Rulers made it impossible to store rations. There was no electricity, so no fridge. The constant heat meant food would not last well. The atmosphere was like a greenhouse.

Today I am going to the office. I'll do my work, and I'll make it home. I'll use extra eye drops. I've had an idea. I'm not going to open the air drop today. See if I can save it till tomorrow. It'll be hard not to eat the food, but I need to see if it's possible. I've spent so many years not trying to find out what, if anything, I can do to escape. Brutal repetitiveness, exhaustion, and the comfort of knowing the air drop will be waiting have shackled us all into submission. If I don't change something, I'll go mad.

It worked. I now have two open air drops, double rations. I was worried the boxes wouldn't open, or I wouldn't get another one the following day. I'm going to do this again to store food for as many days as I can stand. The boxes seem to keep food fresh. Then I'll have food for the road ahead.

We work during daylight. We are only ever on the estate at night. To get a proper look I had to avoid the office today, take my chances. I can only hope the air drops resume once I return to work. I haven't missed a day in all these years. I wait for a clear day and climb onto the roof of the estate. The haze lining the distance makes it so hard to see anything clearly, but I have no choice. This life is not a life.

On the roof I see pure desolation. I've never seen the scale of it before. Despite the heat, it sends shivers through me to see it. The sun beats down on me as I take it all in, squinting heavily. I'm looking for anything. A building rising out of the landscape, a shimmer, an object of some kind to head for, to see how far I can get.

They know where to send the air drops because this was my declared location when the Rulers took all our information. I have never spoken to another Ruler since. If I leave this place no one will know where I am. If I don't find anything out there, I will certainly die. The rations and eye drops will get me so far, and return will be practically impossible once the resources are gone.

I must find something. What would I be returning to anyway? This dilapidated housing, the offices, the work, the dependence on the eye drops to earn my rations. What do I have to lose? Nothing.

After five days of running, working, and starving, I was near broken. So weak it didn't seem possible I would make it a hundred meters, let alone to that towering structure I had glimpsed. It stood like a radio control tower, at its tip a large dish jutting out of the debris. The curve of the Earth limits the distance the human eye can see to around thirty miles from a tall building. I should be able to do that in a day. I remember walking on cliff tops by the sea, decades ago. See that light house? That's where we're walking today. A tiny spec that looked too far away to reach, yet after a few hours, there you are, at the base looking up and enjoying the view out to the ocean.

No office today. This is it.

I got no air drop last night, so I have five small boxes wrapped in a sheet which I've tied round the end of a stick. I look like an apocalyptic Huckleberry Finn. An old shirt wrapped round my head to protect from the sun. I start by going back up to the roof to get my bearings. I can't see the tower today. The skyline is like mud. Browns and oranges curdling together on the horizon. The burning haze hanging low, making the ground wobble and jitter. I can't wait another day for it to be clearer. I have a best effort idea of where I'm heading. Looking straight down I see the wild hanging around outside the estate and beyond. Waiting for people going to and from work.

I hit the eye drops. Two doses in each eye. Might be overkill but I need a good start. Couldn't see any wild beyond the estate so hopefully just need to make it out and into the wilderness. I'll be completely alone, but free.

I run down the stairwell, nearly falling and killing my chances before leaving the building. Managed to catch myself though and only graze my hand on the wall. Once outside I make sure I know the direction and run fast. The stick isn't making it easy, as it wobbles around behind me, so I take each end of it and rest it across my shoulders, elbows out at head height. I clear the estate in around two hours. I can tell from the way the sun has moved.

Now I'm away from it all. Away from that life, and deep in the rubble. No idea what to expect, or who I might see. What if I find the Rulers? They would probably kill me. I need to make the most of the eye drops I used. I stopped thinking and get a steady pace. Sprinting. I can do this for a few hours then fall back to walking the rest. I opened one box for this outward journey. The tiny bites and hydration would be sufficient.

I've reached an old car park.

Rusting relics littered the place, hot tarmac felt through the soles of my shoes. I can see the tower now. Aside from the estates, not a single building seems to have survived whatever it was the Rulers used to sack the cities, apart from this rising metal monument. I could only think it must be a communication tower they had protected. Some piece of vital infrastructure.

Standing at the base of it I got a flashback of being on that cliff. I was at the foot of a beautiful lighthouse. The rolling clouds and crashing waves stretching out for miles ahead of me. This was the most I had felt in a long time. I broke down in tears uncontrollably. I hadn't had an image of my previous life in so long I'd forgotten most of it. This tower brought me closer to myself than I'd been in years.

After sitting and crying I felt an alien wave of joy. A release from the horrors of the estates and the offices. To be out here, alone, and with nothing save my boxes and this tower, I felt hope.

The eyes drops had worn off. The restless feeling in my limbs they created now forced me onto my feet. I touched the tower and made the decision to climb it. It was taller than the estate, I would be able to see further into the distance from the top.

I clung to the poles at its tip. Standing next to the dish. It hummed. There was power running through it from somewhere. From up here I could look back to the estate and even see past it to the offices. I could see further in the direction I was travelling as well. More communication towers. Three in total, in different directions.

It's impossible to make any kind of logical decision on which one to head to. All look the same as this one. Go down the centre I say. At least that way I can see what's on each side.

I spend days doing this. Covering what feels like hundreds of miles. A routine of run, snack, drink, drop, climb. Eventually, I find something incredible. I've climbed a tower and can see a mirage, I think. The heat must be getting to me.

In the centre of a blast radius of flattened buildings stands a lush jungle. It doesn't look real. The burnt sky and scorched landscape, not the right surrounding for vegetation like that. This can't be real. It's like a dream.

More astonishing was the building in the middle of the jungle. A palace. Gold domes, alabaster walls, azul blue finishing's on the features.

If it's real, there is no doubt in my mind this place is where the Rulers live.

I've got a day's rations left. A few days eye drops. I can't even imagine seeing one of those people. What would either of us do? I'd need a big drop before getting anyway near the palace. Fear and anxiety grip me like a vice. Aching temples and tight chest. Not what I want at the top of this tower. I need to get down.

Back on the ground I take a drop. The focus is on me. I need to be able to think and move.

I get a speed going until I'm able to see the edge of the jungle from the ground. I rest, eat half the rations, then dig a sleeping hole. If I see one of those bastards, I don't know what I'll do. Take one with me at least if it comes to it.

I sleep that night and wake up with the first light. I eat and drink the remaining rations, keep the stick but leave everything else here. I'm going into the jungle.

I've just applied all the remaining eye drops. This is the most I've had in one go. Shit. I could run 10k in six minutes right now. My heart and glands beating to an unstoppable rhythm. Fingertips pulsating. I can see rain falling in the jungle. It hasn't rained in twenty years. The dream-like feeling is there. I'm in a strong state of focus, but nothing makes sense. I'm at the edge and have to pass under a sheet of thick clear plastic to enter. I'm instantly sweating with the humidity. I can run my hands along the bark of moist palms and mahogany. Rain lands on my face and I could die now, in sheer bliss. A tropical heaven. A respite from the arid world I came from.

I hear animals. Monkeys, and birds. The trees rustle with movement. A gentle wind washes over me, helping to cool me down.

Great pipework is hidden among the trees. Stems from the ground and emits clouds of steam at frequent intervals. Like a hot house in a botanical garden. A vast system of underground vapour dispensers protrudes out the surface uniformly.

They have made themselves very comfortable here. Everything a person could want. An opulent palace surrounded by a rich habitat. How could they be so cruel, knowing how we live our lives in the estates?

I turn from the wonder of the wildlife to reach the palace. I'm sprinting, every muscle burning. Jumping vines and narrow winding rivers. I swing from low hanging branches. Animal form. Back to my ancient roots. I'm screaming as I run. I must find someone. Must get an answer, regardless of the consequences.

I explode from the jungle on to the lawns. It's like a fantasy. Rhino parades the grounds in herds of eight or more. I have the focus to go straight through them, causing a stampede away from me. Luscious planting is trampled and crushed. Great pots toppled and smashed.

Finally, I reach the palace. A great door. I listen out but the quiet is either hiding the truth or is reflecting the lack of activity inside. The eye drops won't let me stand here long. Pushing the huge door, it swings open easily, revealing an entrance hall. Winding stairs, balconies, and a ceiling so high I begin to sway as I look up.

Not a sound to be heard.

Up the stairs and through gold panelled doors. Artistic interiors. Expensive furniture. Light pouring in through towering windows to illuminate the space and cast atmospheric shadows over the perfectly arranged furnishings.

I use all the energy I have searching the palace. Not a single person, or even a sign of human inhabitants. Just an immaculately preserved pile, fit for a King.

An overwhelming exhaustion is setting in. I'm barely able to stand suddenly. The marble floor is not inviting enough to let myself pass out on the spot. There was a bedroom off the balcony atop the entrance stairs. I need to make it to the bed, now.

Falling up the stairs to the room my peripheral vision is getting darker. My eyes ache so heavily I'm clutching my head. I must've had too many of the drops. Stupid.

I land on the mattress like a sack of vegetables hitting a motorway after falling off the back of a truck. I can't move now I'm down. Consciousness is there. Thought. A semblance of touch, but I'm mostly numb. If anyone came in, I'd be entirely defenceless. Still got the focus, but the body can't cope. I need sleep to come. Take away the worry about my vulnerability. I've never done this before, but I start counting sheep. Lying there, in a Sultan's chambers, catatonic, and softly mouthing with dry lips:

1 sheep.

2 sheep.

3 sheep.

4 sheep.

5 shee...

6 sh...

7...

It's dark when I wake up. I can see the moon through the glass domed ceiling. A feeling of relief that I can move but countered by an intense headache with crippling eye strain. I don't want to stay here. This place is too vast, and too empty. It doesn't feel safe.

Slowly, I'm up on my feet. I leave the room onto the balcony in the entrance hallway and try to get my senses to align. My hearing is muffled. Eyes are sore. Skin is tingling.

No more eye drops left. No more food or drink.

This place must have a kitchen. I need to find it before I leave. I start to descend the grand stairs to see hundreds of people in the hallway, standing in total silence. The loud intake of breath it induces rings out audibly. I'm unable to hide here and running will draw attention. No one has even turned to look. These people do not appear to be moving at all, like wax works.

I get down with them, move through the crowd. Their eyes are wide and lifeless. Every man and woman, vacant. Transfixed on a point only they could see. Being so close I can see the rugged lines of age, the missing patches in facial hair, scars, and veins. Yet not one of them moves. Their eyes still.

I hurry through the crowd to the end of the hallway and make my way through a side door that leads to a long corridor. If I can find a kitchen, fill up with water and maybe find some food, I can leave.

This takes me down into the underbelly of the palace. Tight, dimly lit stairwells. Less regal, more servant's quarters. More claustrophobic. There are no rooms off the corridors, so I just keep following. Eventually I come to a door. A blue, flickering light shines underneath. I try the door, and it opens into a room with a large unit of screens. A table and work bench. Thousands of tiny components are scattered around lazily. The familiarity unmistakable. I built these components in the offices. Small electrical and mechanical gizmos. I never knew why I made them. I just followed the plans left at the bench.

In front of the screens there is a velvet sofa. Each screen shows a room. A room within the estate. I recognise my own pit. The few items of clothing. The old leather chair I sat in, worn to straw in some places.

Horrified, I sit on the sofa staring at the screens.

A side door opens and a man in impeccable butler's dress enters the room carrying a silver tray. Delicately sitting on it is a glass of Champagne and a plate of food. He puts them down in front of me, gives a crooked smile by raising one corner of his mouth, then turns and leaves without a word.

I'm rigid with fear.

Do I consume these offerings? Who does he think I am?

I don't want the drink, but the food looks good. I eat the whole plate in a few mouthfuls, swallowing it in chunks.

I go back out the way I came in at my natural speed. I'll be back in the hall again. I don't like this prospect. Those people aren't right. I get to the little side door and open it. The crowd is up to the door, all of them staring straight at me now. Still motionless but the eyes are alive. Gaping and unblinking. They don't move towards me. I steady myself and walk into them again and hear a small sound. A mechanical whirring. The bodies don't turn to follow me, but the eyes do. Hundreds of pairs of eyes, operated by tiny components, buzzing quietly as they move in eye sockets.

I finally get to the entrance and out into the night. I close the door behind me, and all is silent. My own eyes can't adjust. The night is blurry. Lights flicker in the corner of my vision but disappear as soon as I look at them.

I'm running towards the jungle now, but I can feel my eyesight is going. Either a symptom of dependency or I'm going blind. Colours swim together on a black background. Shapes and forms loom towards me. The trees perhaps. I'm now completely disoriented. I don't know which way I'm facing, or which way I came from.

I can't stop though so I tear onwards to meet my fate.

Without warning I feel someone grab me.

I'm pulled to the ground. There was more than one. I do all I can by kicking and lashing out. Spitting and screaming, trying to use my teeth when I feel them near my face. Slowly I start to lose consciousness. The world goes dark, and I slip away downstream.

It was raining heavily outside. Humidity clung to the air in this unusually warm summer. It was the middle of the night, but no one was sleeping.

Janet O'Conner stood over the hospital bed, looking down at her best friend.

A nurse nearby was checking a clipboard and doing basic checks for vital signs. A woman was laid out on the bed, having sustained a head injury on the way to work when she passed out on the train.

The impact to the back of her head meant she would lose her sight permanently.

After several weeks she was able to sit up on her own. Janet sat beside her in a plastic-coated chair and offered sips of water, or spoonful's of food. Today she was booked in for an experimental procedure to try and recover her sight at least partially. A clinical program needed trial subjects, and the timing was perfect.

Several days after the surgery, they came to the bedside which Janet had not left. She was in the foetal position on the chair. She uncoiled herself and sat up when the doctor cleared his throat.

“Mrs O'Conner?”

“Yes.”

“We believe the operation to have been a success. We will need to monitor carefully of course, but initial response to the treatment is excellent. Her readings are exactly what we would expect. We are going to remove the bandages now. Can I ask you to wake her for us?”

Janet gently stroked the woman's hair, and spoke softly saying the doctor has come, and needs to remove the bandages.

The woman sits up in slow motion. Her entire head wrapped in white. She does as she is asked but never speaks. The doctor explains what he is looking for and asks for consent to approach her to take the bandages off. The woman doesn't respond, so he removes them carefully anyway.

Once the bandages are off, everyone steps back to see the result.

The woman's head is motionless, and she does not make a sound. Two wide eyes track the people in front of her, making a small mechanical whirring sound as they move.

Janet takes a step back. She doesn't recognise the person in front of her anymore.

“What have you done?”

The doctor reaches into his pocket and produces a small bottle.

“Make sure she is using these drops. Twice a day, and she'll be just fine.”

The End
