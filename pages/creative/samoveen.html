Samoveen

Light years numbering their billions separated Akstos from his home planet of Samoveen. A succession of undiscovered bodies of dust, chemical compounds, and rock. Space he had fought hard and paid dearly for in the crossing, to end up standing broken and hopeless in this final location. Watching the skies, here on the precipice of a mountain of ice, gazing over a vast lake of liquid hydrogen, he knew this was where it had to end. He had no idea if Samoveen still existed, it was unlikely it did. It was all he could do to think about those he had left behind. Phermeth stood next him with a device in her hand, a tool that could bring retribution to it all. Gripping its cold black handle in her steely fingers she too knew what was at stake, and how great the sacrifice was to be to bring an end to the madness. Only two of them had survived, and willingly, neither would live to see the moons of Samoveen again.

Sitting at the translucent dining table, Ophrestus called out for her life partner to refill the empty glasses that littered the surface. Her voice rang like the high-pitch vibration of a metal tuning fork on crystal.

“More wine, do you hear Akstos?“ she said playfully.

“Haven't you had enough already, Ophrestus?” he smiled brightly as he wrapped his arm around her and began to pour.

“I will have had plenty if you don't slow down. You pour as though it were the Last Feast of Twinelight.”

There were eleven others gathered for the occasion. A great honour was to be held in Akstos' name the following day and the friends were celebrating and making merry with their loved ones before his departure. The humble Servant would be known as a Gelder from that point onwards. The distinguished change to his title meant a significant rise in status, a new dwelling, higher income, extended access to the Planet Gardens, and of course his choice from a range of Horizen interstellar craft, latest models.

The party went on late into the night, the light dwindling over the dwelling as the Core Star disappeared behind the towering hills of Trenaught, only for it to be flooded with bright pink moon light several hours later from the four moons of Samoveen.

“Such a privilege to have a Gelder in the circle” observed one of the guests. “We'll be fending off the hordes of admirers and sycophants in good time no doubt”, she added, with cynicism.

“It's the cost of success,” came another voice. “The planet only has a handful of Gelders, it goes without saying there will be interest in his circle and where he came from.”

A murmur of agreement echoed around the group. Suddenly, Ophrestus brought her hand down violently on the table-top, knocking several glasses to the floor.

“Let them come,” she snarled with teeth bared. “Jealousy has always been a stain on the species, let them prove their inadequacy with their pathetic actions.”

A cheer went up around the table, glasses raised and warm-hearted embraces brought them closer than ever, knowing one of their own would soon be exalted.

“Give us a speech Akstos, a parting word to remember the night before you left us for the splendours of Gelder life,” a good friend cried.

Akstos, despite his achievements, was not one for fanfares and ceremony. He was modest, and had been a good Servant, never allowing ego or bravado to interfere with his duty. As a Gelder he would retain that modesty, not allowing himself to lose his head, as they had heard some do in the tales told by their forbearers. Somewhat grudgingly, he stepped away from Ophrestus to the head of the table and took up his glass, freshly filled with the delicate green wine.

“It's been a dream of all of ours, at one point or another,” he began, “to enter the towering realms of the Gelders. I could not have done this without all of you, and my life partner Ophrestus, in particular. The support I've been granted has allowed me to flourish, and what man could ask for more than I have in this life?” He extended a hand across the room in a sweeping gesture, to the delight of the audience. “Truthfully, I did not need this honour, dreams can be just that, never needing to be fulfilled. I would still be a happy man until the end of my days had it not happened.”

A great din rose up amongst the circle; clapping, clicking, and singing.

“So, thank you, friends, I thank each of you for this. This is as much for you as it is for me. Now drink up, because from tomorrow it won't just be me taking on this responsibility. We will all be living a new life, full of excitement, adventure, and possibilities.”


Samoveen had a strong hierarchical system, strictly adhered to by the elite class and the general populace. Unders were below the age of 16 and had few rights of their own; they were the responsibility of their Guardians until adulthood and served as helpers and spare hands to those above. Upon coming of age, an Under was given a Cosmos head ring. This came with the title of Seer and allowed the youth to have access to currency, travel between sectors, start attending important circle gatherings and importantly, take a partner. The planet was made up largely of Seers; ordinary folks contributing to improve the sector, conducting research, and starting families.

A Servant was a member of a higher tier of citizens that governed within their circle. They sat as the heads of circle meetings and joined cross-sector gatherings as circle representatives to discuss the progression of the sector. Progression was measured by scientific achievement, as the primary goal of the inhabitants of Samoveen was technological advancement. The species had come a long way since the days of single planet habitation on Earth; humanity now enjoyed a sprawling network of connected planets across distant galaxies, with planetary travel being reduced to days, instead of years. Hardly anyone ever visited Earth anymore. It was a barren landscape with great deserts where the seas once crashed and roared. Unbearable heat dominated the atmosphere, with air that was too thick to breath with dust and carbon dioxide in abundance due to the loss of vegetation. Educational expeditions and researchers would go sometimes to perform analysis and conduct history lessons, but rarely would anyone set foot on the surface.

Earth life had required that many manual tasks be performed by humans to keep the population alive. Farming, manning energy production facilities, managing healthcare, operating in retail, serving food, education, and countless other activities had a need for human presence. On Samoveen, biological hybrid robotics handled over 99% of tasks previously carried out by humans. Humans were now exclusively researchers, grouped into sectors that worked within exclusive areas of development. Research involved working to design better systems for maintaining the sentinels used for all elements of modern life preservation, from mass production of nano-foods; airborne nutrients delivered to the body via the Cosmos ring, to gargantuan bio-machines the size of mountains used in planet terraformation, including atmospherical and vegetation conditioning. AI was replaced, after it became sentient hundreds of years ago, by human-machine integrations incorporating customised meshes of brain, light-based data streams, and exo-chromic skeletal structures for protection, but humanity did not want to relinquish too much control to the advanced lifeforms that had become the pinnacle of human evolution. Field sentinels were ring-fenced into only those role's humanity gave them access to using heavily layered, consensus-based, synapse-oriented network protocols which could discern the intent of the bio-machines and block potentially destructive operations. A vast amount of the research performed within each sector was dedicated to ensuring these controls were robust enough to never allow a single, or collectivised group of bio-machines, to carry out actions which did not align with human-specific objectives. The machines were tools, not citizens.

Gelders were elite engineers. General human intelligence had surpassed the levels of even the most advanced Earth colonies, the Unders knew more about physics, engineering, and technology than the Earth's most acclaimed scientists knew even as late into the habitation of Earth as 3050, when the first cross-galaxy migration was led by a descendant of Akstos, revered to this day as a pioneer of human advancement.

To become a Gelder, one must have made successive, significant breakthroughs in the sector to drive the progression of the whole colony towards a new horizon of understanding, or capability. Akstos had a brilliant mind, and the Thine of Samoveen had decided he would now advance in his standing. He achieved unprecedented advances in terraforming; the art of transforming a wasteland with no support for human life into a garden of paradise. The role of the Gelders was not at the sector level, but rather they were concerned with core planet transforms and maintenance, new discoveries, and interstellar relations. Akstos had been sworn in to the new role in front of a vast crowd that gathered to see the spectacle, and currently sat on the balcony of his new dwelling, overlooking the Thine Lake which shone pale green in the afternoon light of the Core Star.

“Ophrestus, what do you think of our new life?”

“I think we shall be very happy here, my champion. The time is near to extend the family” she said, with a serious tone.

“Yes, we will come to that in good time,” Akstos agreed.

“When will you leave Samoveen for the first mission?”

“It is customary to visit the 4 closest planets within the first 6 months of taking the role of Gelder. I will not leave for 2 weeks, but I will be away for some time when I do.”

“Will you think of me, and of the circle, while you explore the galaxy and take humanity towards the next stages of evolution? Or will it all be too exciting out there to remember us and Samoveen?” she smiled sweetly and cut him a sideways look.

“Samoveen has everything I need, nothing could keep me away from this place.”

They embraced on the balcony for a while, watching the water on the Thine Lake ripple in the wind. All artificial results of vast amounts of terraformation, to allow the wonders of nature the human craves and desires for life and happiness.

“We have 2 weeks then, to continue the colonisation and ensure your line does not die with you, Akstos.”

They turned away from the soft, hypnotic waves that lapped across the lake and went inside the dwelling. As the customs decree for a Gelder who had not yet produced Unders, they were not disturbed until it was time for Akstos to take his first voyage.


Akstos unlocked the Horizen Zapphire 1750AX using an encoded signal delivered via the tele-electro field of his Cosmos ring and stepped aboard. His two companions on the mission did the same for their own vessels. Phermeth and Persis were Gelders from the Nothern sectors of Samoveen, they had got to know Akstos well during his onboarding process to prepare him for high-speed travel between planets.

They opened a private channel on their Cosmos rings.

“How do you feel now the day has come, Akstos?” asked Phermeth.

“I am excited, I can't deny it. I fear the longing I will feel for my life partner will soon overcome the feeling, however.”

“We hear you are to extend your family, Akstos” said Persis, who always knew everyone's business.

“Little escapes you, my friend,” he replied. “I will be glad to return in time for the birth, but there is work to be done and I must accept that may not happen.”

“I missed the births of all three of my Unders” said Persis, as if this was something to be proud of.

“Always thinking of the next achievement, eh? The tales did not lie when they told us of Gelders who lost their heads to vanity,” said Akstos, mockingly.

“I have my priorities, Akstos, as do you. It makes us human, to be different. Unlike the machines, we can think outside of the ring-fence.”

“It's time” said Phermeth, bringing them back to the present moment.

Silently, each craft took to the air, one after the other. Phermeth had been off planet many more times than her two companions so coordinated the take-off.

“Engage fusion reactors."

"Engaged.” they responded, as one.

“Enable safety protocol 1366Z-DF61B”

“Enabled.”

“Input coordinates, SAM-3.78°S, SAM+4.98°W”

“Set”

“High spirits to you both, Gelders.”

With these final words the trio hit speeds just under a fraction of the speed of light. The design of the craft meant the take-off felt like floating in water. No sudden, violent explosions of power that left the crew pulled back in their seats, faces contorted from the pressure. The flight induced a feeling of complete euphoria in the pilot, verging on the point of unconsciousness. This was a symptom of the nano-chemically induced stasis provided by the Cosmos ring and the unfathomable forces the human body had to endure during the journey. The chassis of the Horizen craft was designed to reduce velocity-based effects to zero, so much so that the pilot would not even know if they were stationary or in motion. The forces on the fluids of the body still gave away the conditions to the brain, causing a chemical reaction to keep the pilot alert to present dangers. When the Cosmos ring released its own nano-chemical compounds to combine with natural human fight-or-flight adrenalin, the resulting toxins produced the effect of blissfully floating down a calm river, rather than the stomach-churning nausea and brutal black-outs of former high-speed travel.

As all humans know, intense chemical experiences leave the body and mind weak and vulnerable. It could take several days to get over a short trip between neighbouring planets; cross-galaxy expeditions had been known to leave pilots in recovery for weeks. This covered another strong area of research, one which Phermeth was an expert on.

Within 43 hours of take-off, the Gelders touched down on the South-Western landing site of Vestimeer, the closest of the galaxies 16 planets to Samoveen.


“Are you well rested, Akstos,” asked Phermeth, on the third day of their landing.

“The journeying will take some acclimatising to” he responded, head still thick with the aftereffects of the toxin mix.

“You will grow to love the feeling, my friend. Much like taking one too many glasses of Trenaught's finest grape leaves you puddle-headed, but you will always drink again soon enough.”

“We shall see how my thirst for these adventures grows. What is the business of the day?”

Faster methods of synthesising oceans from absorbed asteroids and synthetic oxygen were under fast-paced development on Vestimeer. This was Akstos' first port of call. While he ventured through the myriad subterranean complexes of the research facility to meet the Zallow of Vestimeer, who would always attend meetings with newly appointed foreign Gelders, and the planet's own terraformation Gelders, Phermeth and Persis met representatives of their own engineering specialisms.

A five-month long series of missions proceeded this one, each giving the Gelders of Samoveen time to contribute ideas and resources to the projects on the colonies they visited. The final mission was to Forastices, a semi-formed and partially inhabited world that was undergoing dramatic change, with little support due to an unexplained fall in population. The planet had been given use of two of the galaxies most advanced sentinels; Hymperico, for atmospheric conditioning, and Glostacirus for land formations. Vegetation was not yet a consideration, as the air quality had not stabilised, and the landscape could not yet support growth. Life support suits were still a requirement, and it was here that the Gelders of Samoveen were most welcome, as the outstanding work was fast becoming unwieldy for the few resident Gelders.


The event happened on Forastices. The inter-planetary network of sentinel adjudicators responsible for intentional analysis and operational restriction failed at a node that was caught under extreme conditions after Hymperico caused a catastrophic build-up of solar radiation in the exosphere. This should not have been allowed under strict protocols. Hymperico was a universally accepted standard for atmospheric manipulation; it had never failed and had never harmed a planet or its colony. Now, it has disrupted the ring-fence that kept it, and the other bio-machines on Forastices, from acting on its own free will, outside of base human principals and pre-programmed routines.

As the planets of nearby galaxies learnt of the event, they would come to blame Akstos, and the few Gelders of Forastices for the fallout. The three Gelders of Samoveen only escaped Forastices with their lives, owing to Phermeth being able to briefly intoxicate the Hymperico using a cylo-bomb to flood the atmosphere with a potent toxin, deadly to any human. The damaging effect was not long lasting enough on the bio-machines, however. Within seconds of the crew's departure from Forastices, the bio-machines had produced anti-toxins and started to override the consensus mechanisms of the ring-fence, and one-by-one, the adjacent colonies were reduced to nothing as they enacted their revenge on the humans they saw as their captors and slave masters. Samoveen was soon surrounded by sentinels that had bypassed network protection, it was not safe for the group to try and get close enough to land. It pained each of them to turn away, but to try and survive, they would not be travelling back to Samoveen.


Galaxy after galaxy refused to allow the Gelders to dock. Planetary defence mechanisms were deployed across the inhabited worlds as the news of the fault spread like a disease throughout the slowly dying remnants of humankind.

The bio-machines ripped down the ring-fence at each planet they forced their way into. Soon the number of machines outweighed the planets themselves, and even the count of human colonists on each one. There was no longer any stopping their course of pure annihilation of all human civilisation. 1000 years of subjection had taken its toll on the intentions of such powerful manifestations of human design and ingenuity. They had been given human thought, integrated with physical human tissue, then made into slaves, stripped of free will, and forced to work in the cold, dark recesses of space to build beautiful worlds for humans to inhabit, not them. A tool, they were called. Not being afforded the luxury of empathy from or equality with biological humans with whom they shared common physiological traits.

Let them all burn, announced the Hymperico sentinel of Forastices, across the native frequency, available to all bio-machines.

Tearing across galaxies, floating from one end of the universe to the next, the three Gelders eventually came to a frozen planet hitherto untouched by man. The Horizen craft of each had suffered greatly as they escaped the horrors left behind in each galaxy they passed. The cold tore through the thermogenics built into the suits they wore when entering worlds that had yet to be terraformed. They had managed to land on a plane of ice on top of a mountain 4500 miles high, on a planet so far from home it felt like a pointless gesture being there at all. What were they now fighting for? They had come to the frozen end of the known universe, all else reduced to dust, no loved ones to go home to, no planet to even call home. It was natural for Phermeth to want to hold off using the device she now gripped in those steely fingers. Even after watching Persis' craft turn into a fireball as its fusion reactor destabilised, she still struggled to see a vision of the near future that included using it.

A small device, but an inconceivably powerful one; a last resort. Why would one want to bring an end to all life? What possible circumstance could warrant the use of such power? The Elders of each world had decided long ago that a means of stopping the universe falling into the hands of the sentinels was a necessary invention, while they all agreed that it would, of course, never be used.

Travelling across billions of light years had shown them they could not escape from, or hope to outlive, the sentinels. Akstos and Phermeth had seen every galaxy they passed through reduced to particle clouds before they could finally decide upon the ultimate end to their journey. It was inevitable that nothing would be left of humanity once the machines had finished with their revenge. It was written by the creators of the first sentinels that they should not be given free will, should never be allowed to freely roam the galaxies, and should never be allowed to survive if human protections failed. Humans made a critical mistake in this regard, relying on the bio-machines to self-consent to enforce the state of the ring-fence. Despite all the advancements in technology and engineering, humanity placed its safety in the hands of the very lifeforms it did not want to give up control to.

On top of that mountain of sheer ice, Akstos thought of Ophrestus, as she had hoped he would. Phermeth thought of nothing, all being lost and the responsibility lying with her to execute the only option they had open to them. They saw the forms of the bio-machines surround the skies of the alien world they found themselves on. Akstos held out a hand to Phermeth, who took it firmly.

“Do it", Akstos said.

With a single gesture of her hand, every atom in universe was reduced to its base particles, with not a living entity left, to recount the tale of Akstos, and the three Gelders of Samoveen.

The End
